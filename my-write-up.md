# Write-Up: Daily Reflection Tree
## Design Rationale

---

### Why These Questions?

Designing these questions was harder than I expected — not because the psychology was complicated, but because the obvious questions felt fake. "Did you take ownership today?" is a useless question. Anyone can say yes. The challenge was finding questions that made a person pause and answer honestly, even when the honest answer wasn't flattering.

I started from my own experience. I thought about a time I had planned something and it didn't happen — I had built it up in my head, prepared for it, and then at the last moment it didn't come together. My first reaction wasn't blame. It was "okay, maybe next time." And then, shortly after, a second thought: "but was there something I could have done differently?" That gap between those two thoughts — the acceptance and the self-questioning — is exactly what Axis 1 is trying to surface. So that became the first question: *when something you planned didn't work out, what was your first thought?*

For Axis 2, I thought about two moments. One was on a bus — I noticed a child with a skin condition standing while everyone else stayed seated. I felt that the child may fall. So, I gave him my seat. No one asked me to. No one acknowledged it. I didn't need them to.I just felt good after doing that. The second was in college — a friend asked me to submit his assignment.Another friend questioned why I would do that — he said, 'when he wasn't there for you, why are you doing this?' My answer was simple: he asked, I said yes, what he would do for me isn't my calculation to make. Both moments pointed to the same thing — contribution that doesn't run a ledger. That's what the Axis 2 question tries to find: *what was going through your mind when you helped?*

For Axis 3, I noticed something about myself. In arguments, I don't just hold my position — I actually stop and think, "wait, maybe they're right." And more broadly, I find I often care more about how my actions affect others than how they affect me. That's not noble, it's just how I'm wired. But I realized that's actually what this axis is measuring — whether someone's awareness extends beyond themselves. So Axis 3 became: *in a disagreement, what were you mostly thinking about?*

---

### How I Designed the Branching

The tree branches on two things: what answer the person selects (for routing between question paths) and what signals have accumulated (for routing to the final reflection).

The key trade-off I made was between depth and honesty. I could have asked five questions per axis, but a tired person at the end of the day will click through without thinking if the session feels too long. Two questions per axis felt like the right limit — enough to cross-check the first answer, not enough to exhaust the person.

I also tried to make the options genuinely hard to distinguish. The worst version of this kind of tool has one obviously "good" answer and three obviously "bad" ones. Real people don't think that way. Someone who says "I accepted it and kept going" after a plan fell apart isn't being passive — that can be a form of resilience. I tried to write options where the person has to actually think about which one fits, rather than just picking the one that sounds best.

The reflections are the part I spent most time on. The assignment said "no moralizing" — and that was the hardest constraint. My instinct was to write encouraging conclusions. But encouragement that isn't earned is just flattery. So I tried to write reflections that name what happened, honestly, without judgment. The external locus reflection doesn't say "you were wrong" — it says "noticing is where agency begins." That's still a push, but a gentle one.

### Psychological Sources

**Axis 1 — Locus of Control (Rotter, 1954):** People with an internal locus believe their actions shape outcomes. Those with an external locus attribute outcomes to luck, others, or circumstances. The questions try to surface this without leading — "it wasn't in my control" is a legitimate answer, not automatically a wrong one.

**Axis 2 — Organizational Citizenship Behavior (Organ, 1988) and Psychological Entitlement (Campbell et al., 2004):** OCB describes discretionary effort that goes beyond formal job requirements — helping a colleague, doing something unglamorous, volunteering without being asked. Entitlement is the belief that one deserves more than others, regardless of contribution. The bus moment and the assignment moment from my own life are both examples of OCB — helping without calculation.

**Axis 3 — Self-Transcendence (Maslow, 1969) and Perspective-Taking (Batson, 2011):** Maslow's later work described a level beyond self-actualization — orienting outward, toward what the world needs rather than what you need. Batson's work on perspective-taking describes the cognitive act of imagining another's experience. The Axis 3 questions try to find whether the person's circle of awareness extends beyond themselves.

---

### What I Would Improve With More Time

**More cross-axis connections.** The tree currently treats each axis independently. A more sophisticated version would reference earlier answers in later reflections — "you said you felt stuck when plans changed, but you also helped someone without being asked. That tension is worth sitting with." This requires more state and more reflection templates, but it would make the conversation feel much more like a real dialogue.

**Better entitlement detection.** Entitlement is the hardest thing to surface because it's invisible to the person holding it. My current Axis 2 questions are decent but not sharp enough. Someone can read "I felt others should have done it instead of me" and genuinely not recognise themselves in it, even if that's exactly their pattern. With more time I would test these options against real people and iterate.

**A longitudinal view.** The most useful version of this tool wouldn't just reflect on today — it would show you your own pattern over 30 days. "This month you've been leaning external on agency. Same as last quarter during the deadline crunch." That's when the tool becomes genuinely useful rather than just interesting.
